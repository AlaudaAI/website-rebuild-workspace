Filing for Divorce in Tennessee

The divorce rate in Tennessee is 2.9 per 1000 residents. While these numbers are relatively low, many residents file for divorce every day for a variety of reasons. Tennessee is a no-fault divorce state, which means a spouse doesn’t have to legally prove their partner did something wrong to end their marriage. However, spouses do have the right to cite specific grounds for divorce if their partner harmed them or the relationship in some way.

If you file a no-fault divorce, then you’re acknowledging that neither spouse is to blame. When you file for a no-fault divorce, you must choose one of the following grounds to move forward:

  • Irreconcilable Differences. This is the most common issue couples cite when filing for divorce in Tennessee. It acknowledges that the marriage is beyond repair.
  • Separated for Two Years. If a couple has been separated and uninvolved for two or more years, this demonstrates that the marriage is beyond repair.

A fault-based divorce could be chosen for a variety of reasons, but they require proof when filing for the divorce. In Tennessee, the following reasons could be grounds for divorce:

  • Impotence or sterility
  • Adultery
  • Bigamy
  • Desertion
  • Felony conviction
  • Domestic violence
  • Excessive alcohol or drug use

To move forward with the divorce in Tennessee, you must meet the residency requirement and wait the mandatory waiting period, which is 60 days, or 90 days if there are minor children involved.

Dividing Property in a La Vergne Divorce

Property division in a divorce can be a point of major contention for many couples. Having a La Vergne divorce attorney on your side can increase your chances of your voice being heard and protecting the assets that are most important to you. Tennessee is not a community property state, which means that instead of dividing marital property 50/50, the court tries to divide property equitably, according to equitable distribution laws.

Joint debts are also considered during this process. The courts usually begin with a 50/50 split and then review other factors, like how long the marriage lasted and what each spouse contributed, financially and non-financially, to determine what each spouse is responsible for.

FAQs

How Much Does It Cost to Get a Divorce in Tennessee?

How much a divorce might cost in Tennessee depends on multiple factors, the most important being the complexity of the divorce. In Tennessee, the average filing fees are between $180-250, but that doesn’t include lawyer fees, court fees, and other additional costs. A straightforward divorce without any contesting will likely cost less than one that is contentious and requires many court proceedings.

What Is the Wife Entitled to in a Divorce in Tennessee?

How much a wife is entitled to in a Tennessee divorce varies depending on the circumstances of the marital property and of the divorce. Tennessee isn’t a community property state, which means that marital property is divided equitably. A judge doesn’t divide the property equally or based on gender, but instead takes into account the needs of both parties, along with the contributions each party made, how long the marriage lasted, and more.

Can I Get a Divorce in Tennessee Without Going to Court?

In Tennessee, you may be able to avoid going to court if you and your spouse agree to file an uncontested divorce. This type of divorce is fairly straightforward and requires both spouses to agree on all divorce terms outside of court. A judge must then review the agreement they created, and if it is approved, the divorce can be settled without further litigation or court proceedings. In a contested divorce, litigation is required to come to a decision on each divorce matter.

What Is the Difference Between a Contested and an Uncontested Divorce in Tennessee?

The main difference between a contested and uncontested divorce in Tennessee is that a contested divorce requires a couple to go to court to secure decisions on divorce matters, whereas an uncontested divorce does not go through court proceedings. In an uncontested divorce, a couple works together to create a divorce agreement that they feel is fair. In a contested divorce, the couple presents their sides in court, and a judge decides on matters for them.
