# Understanding Child Custody Laws in Tennessee: A Comprehensive Guide

Tennessee's child custody laws are designed to prioritize the best interests of the child, recognizing the importance of a stable and strong relationship with both parents. The state's laws distinguish between two types of custody: Physical custody, which determines the child's place of residence, and legal custody, which grants authority to make significant decisions regarding the child's upbringing, including education, healthcare, and religious upbringing. Notably, Tennessee law does not discriminate based on gender, and the court considers various factors when determining custody arrangements.

The state also acknowledges the concept of joint custody, where both parents share physical and legal responsibilities for raising the child. Joint custody can encompass joint physical custody, joint legal custody, or a combination of both. In cases where joint custody is not viable, the court designates a primary residential parent, who assumes the majority of parenting time, and establishes a parenting plan outlining the visitation schedule for the non-residential parent.

Ultimately, Tennessee's **[child custody lawyers](https://borotnlaw.com/practice-areas/divorce-and-family-law/)** aim to prioritize the child's best interests and facilitate meaningful relationships between both parents and the child.

## 1. Types of Custody Arrangements in Tennessee

### Joint Custody Arrangements

Joint physical custody allows the child to spend a significant amount of time with both parents, and both parents have input into important decisions about the child's upbringing. Joint legal custody, on the other hand, enables both parents to make decisions about the child's education, healthcare, and other important matters.

### Sole Custody Arrangements

In sole custody arrangements, one parent has primary physical and legal custody of the child. The non-custodial parent may still have visitation rights, but they do not have decision-making authority.

### Split Custody Arrangements

In some cases, the court may award split custody, where each parent has primary physical custody of at least one of the children involved. This arrangement is less common and is typically only considered when it is in the best interests of each child to be primarily with a different parent.

## 2. Factors Considered in Determining Child Custody in Tennessee

When determining child custody in Tennessee, the court will consider a variety of factors to ensure that the best interests of the child are prioritized. Some of the key factors that the court will consider include the emotional ties between each parent and the child, each parent's ability to provide for the child's needs, the stability of each parent's home environment, and the mental and physical health of each parent. The court will also consider each parent's willingness to facilitate a relationship between the child and the other parent, as well as any history of domestic violence or abuse.

Additionally, the court will consider the child's own wishes if they are old enough to express a preference, as well as any siblings or other significant relationships that may be impacted by the custody arrangement. Overall, Tennessee law is designed to ensure that the best interests of the child are prioritized in all custody decisions, and the court will carefully consider a range of factors to determine the most appropriate arrangement for each family.

## 3. Legal Process for Establishing Child Custody in Tennessee

The legal process for establishing **[child custody in Tennessee](https://borotnlaw.com/choose-the-right-divorce-lawyer-in-tennessee/)** typically begins with one parent filing a petition for custody with the court. Once the petition is filed, both parents will have an opportunity to present evidence and arguments to support their desired custody arrangement. If an agreement cannot be reached through negotiation or mediation, the court will ultimately make a determination based on the best interests of the child.

In some cases, temporary custody orders may be issued while the case is pending, and a guardian ad litem may be appointed to represent the child's interests in court. Ultimately, the court will issue a final order outlining the custody arrangement, including a parenting plan that details visitation schedules and decision-making authority. It is important for parents to seek legal representation to navigate this process and ensure that their rights and interests are protected throughout.

## 4. Modifying Child Custody Arrangements in Tennessee

In some cases, circumstances may change after a custody arrangement has been established, necessitating a modification of the existing order. In Tennessee, either parent can petition the court for a modification if there has been a significant change in circumstances that warrants a change in custody. Some common reasons for seeking a modification include a change in one parent's living situation, a change in work schedule that impacts parenting time, or concerns about a parent's ability to provide a safe and stable environment for the child.

When considering a modification of custody arrangements, the court will once again prioritize the best interests of the child and consider a range of factors to determine whether a modification is warranted. It is important for parents to seek legal guidance when pursuing a modification to ensure that their case is presented effectively and that their rights are protected throughout the process. In conclusion, understanding child custody laws in Tennessee is essential for parents navigating this complex legal process.

By familiarizing themselves with the types of custody arrangements available, the factors considered in determining custody, and the legal process for establishing and modifying custody arrangements, parents can better advocate for their rights and prioritize their child's best interests. With careful consideration and legal guidance, parents can work towards establishing a custody arrangement that supports their child's well-being and allows both parents to maintain meaningful relationships with their children.
